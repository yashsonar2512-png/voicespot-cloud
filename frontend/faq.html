Everything you need to know about setting up, running, and using VoiceSpot.

Model & Accuracy

VoiceSpot supports 8 keywords: yes, no, up, down, left, right, stop, and go. These were chosen from Google's Speech Commands dataset for their practical utility in voice-controlled applications.

The CNN model achieves approximately 92–95% accuracy on the validation set. Real-world accuracy depends on microphone quality, background noise, and how clearly you speak. Speaking in a quiet environment close to the microphone gives the best results.

The model expects a mel-spectrogram of shape (124, 193, 1) — 124 mel frequency bands over 193 time frames, representing 1 second of audio at 16kHz. The spectrogram is computed using n_fft=512, hop_length=83, and fmax=8000.

Audio & Recording

VoiceSpot records audio directly from your microphone using the browser's MediaRecorder API. This requires explicit permission. The audio is only sent to your local backend at localhost:5000 and is never transmitted to any external server.

The model is trained on 1-second clips. Click the microphone, say your keyword clearly, and click stop — all within about 1 second. If you record longer, only the first second is used. Short, crisp recordings outperform long, rambling ones.

The browser's MediaRecorder API defaults to audio/webm format on most browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Edge). VoiceSpot's backend handles this automatically by using ffmpeg to decode the webm before processing. This is why ffmpeg must be installed.

Features

Record a voice clip, type your name, and click Enroll. VoiceSpot extracts a 40-coefficient MFCC vector from your audio and stores it as your voice "fingerprint". Enrolling 3+ times improves accuracy as the system averages your embeddings for a better voice profile.

Alert level reflects the model's confidence: Level 1 (below 50%) is very uncertain, Level 2 (50–69%) is low confidence, Level 3 (70–84%) is medium, Level 4 (85–94%) is high, and Level 5 (95%+) is maximum confidence. Higher alert levels mean the model is more certain about the detected keyword.

All predictions are saved in a SQLite database at backend/predictions.db. Each record stores the timestamp, keyword, confidence score, speaker name, and alert level. You can view aggregated stats per keyword in the Analytics tab.

Troubleshooting Errors

Check the terminal where app.py is running for a full traceback. Common causes: (1) ffmpeg not installed — audio can't be decoded. (2) Shape mismatch — spectrogram dimensions don't match model input. (3) Model not loaded — check that keyword_model.keras exists in the model/ folder.

ffmpeg is not installed or not found in PATH. Install it from gyan.dev, add it to C:\ffmpeg\bin, and add that path to your system's PATH environment variable. Alternatively, add os.environ["PATH"] = r"C:\ffmpeg\bin" + os.pathsep + os.environ.get("PATH", "") at the top of app.py.

The spectrogram shape doesn't match what the model was trained with. Check your model's input shape with print(model.input_shape) and update N_MELS, HOP_LENGTH, and N_TIME in app.py to match. The hard-clamp in _make_spectrogram() handles off-by-one differences automatically.
